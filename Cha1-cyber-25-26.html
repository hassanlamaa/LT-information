Lesson 1: Cybersecurity Foundations

Core Principles and Application Domains

Cyberspace and Security

Understanding Cyberspace

Cyberspace extends our natural environment, reflecting society through digital infrastructures, data and networked services. Unlike physical spaces, it's a pure creation of humans that doesn't belong to nature.

Real-World Example

Social Media Platforms: Facebook, Twitter, and LinkedIn create digital social environments that mirror our physical social interactions but with different rules and risks.

E-commerce: Amazon and eBay have created digital marketplaces that function similarly to physical shopping malls but with global reach and different security challenges.

The DIC Criteria

Fundamental Security Principles

The DIC criteria form the foundation of cybersecurity:

  • Availability (D) - Resources must be operational and accessible when needed
  • Integrity (I) - Resources remain intact, accurate and unmodified without authorization
  • Confidentiality (C) - Data is protected against unauthorized disclosure

Practical Examples

Availability Failure: In 2016, the Dyn DNS outage took down major websites including Twitter, Netflix, and Reddit for hours, demonstrating the importance of availability.

Integrity Breach: The 2014 Sony Pictures hack resulted in modified data and leaked confidential information, showing integrity and confidentiality failures.

Confidentiality Compromise: The 2017 Equifax breach exposed personal data of 147 million people, a massive confidentiality failure.

Additional Security Functions

Beyond the Basics

Modern cybersecurity extends beyond the DIC criteria to include:

  • Identification and Authentication - Verifying user identities
  • Non-repudiation and Accountability - Ensuring actions can't be denied
  • Traceability and Auditing - Tracking activities for analysis

Case Study: Online Banking Security

Modern banking systems implement all these security functions:

  • Authentication: Two-factor authentication using phones
  • Non-repudiation: Digital signatures on transactions
  • Traceability: Complete audit trails of all transactions

When a customer disputes a transaction, the bank can provide undeniable proof through these security measures.

Key Takeaway

Cybersecurity begins with understanding the fundamental principles of availability, integrity, and confidentiality, and extends to physical protection of infrastructure. These principles apply to all digital systems regardless of their specific purpose or technology.

Lesson 2: Technical Security Measures

Implementation and Operational Practices

Logical Security

Software-Based Protection

Implementation of security mechanisms through software to ensure proper functioning of programs, services and data protection:

  • Quality software development and security testing
  • Cryptography for integrity and confidentiality
  • Access control and authentication procedures
  • Malware and intrusion detection systems

Real-World Example: HTTPS Encryption

When you visit a secure website (indicated by HTTPS and a lock icon), logical security measures are at work:

  • Encryption: TLS/SSL protocols encrypt data between your browser and the server
  • Authentication: Digital certificates verify the website's identity
  • Integrity: Hash functions ensure data isn't modified in transit

Application Security

Security by Design

Security integrated throughout the software development lifecycle:

  • Development methodology following standards
  • Application robustness and quality controls
  • Security mechanism integration
  • Regular security updates and patches

Case Study: The Equifax Breach

In 2017, Equifax suffered a massive data breach exposing personal information of 147 million people. The cause was an unpatched vulnerability in the Apache Struts framework.

Security Failures:

  • Failure to apply a known security patch for months
  • Inadequate vulnerability scanning and detection
  • Poor segmentation of sensitive data

This case highlights the critical importance of application security practices, especially timely patching.

Operational Security

Maintaining Secure Operations

Ensuring proper functioning of computer systems and telecommunications networks through:

  • Computer park management
  • Configuration and update management
  • Incident management and resolution
  • Backup, continuity, and maintenance

Real-World Example: Ransomware Attack Response

When the City of Atlanta was hit by ransomware in 2018, their operational security practices were tested:

  • Backup systems: Some departments could restore from backups
  • Gaps identified: Inconsistent backup practices across departments
  • Recovery time: Some systems took weeks to restore
  • Cost: Recovery cost over $2.7 million

Key Insight

Global security is always that of the weakest link. Implementing encryption for data transfer is ineffective if the data can be accessed through unsecured platforms. A comprehensive approach must address all layers of the technology stack.

Holistic Approach and Strategic Implementation

Cyber Threat Levels

Understanding Threat Severity

Cybersecurity threats can be categorized by their potential impact:

  • Low Danger: Nuisances like spam, minor disruptions
  • Medium Danger: Controllable impacts requiring significant resources
  • High Danger: Severe damages with lasting consequences

Case Study: NotPetya Ransomware Attack

In 2017, the NotPetya ransomware attack caused over $10 billion in damages worldwide, targeting companies like Maersk, Merck, and FedEx.

Threat Characteristics:

  • High Impact: Paralyzed operations for major corporations
  • Global Reach: Affected organizations in multiple countries
  • Rapid Spread: Used multiple infection vectors including a compromised software update

This attack demonstrated how a high-danger threat could disrupt global supply chains and cause massive financial losses.

Global Cyber Risks

Beyond Technical Threats

Modern organizations face cybersecurity challenges that extend beyond technical vulnerabilities:

  • Dependence on foreign technology suppliers
  • Economic intelligence and corporate espionage
  • Environmental impacts of digital infrastructure
  • Regulatory compliance across jurisdictions

Real-World Example: SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack

In 2020, hackers compromised SolarWinds' software build system, inserting malware into updates that were then distributed to 18,000 customers, including government agencies.

Global Risk Factors:

  • Supply Chain Vulnerability: Attack through a trusted software supplier
  • Attribution Challenges: Sophisticated state-sponsored actors
  • Trust Erosion: Compromised trust in software update mechanisms

This incident highlighted how global dependencies create systemic risks that transcend individual organizational security.

Security Architecture Dimensions

Holistic Security Framework

Effective cybersecurity requires addressing multiple dimensions simultaneously:

  • Political & Managerial: Security policy, governance, risk management
  • Technical: Security tools, systems, infrastructure
  • Human: Training, awareness, ethics, competence
  • Legal & Regulatory: Compliance, standards, procedures

Case Study: Target Data Breach (2013)

Target suffered a massive data breach exposing 40 million credit cards and 70 million customer records.

Multi-dimensional Failure Analysis:

  • Technical: Hackers entered through HVAC vendor credentials
  • Managerial: Security team warnings were ignored
  • Human: Vendor access management was inadequate
  • Legal: Resulted in $18.5 million multi-state settlement

This case demonstrates how failures across multiple dimensions can compound to create a major security incident.

Course Conclusion

Cybersecurity requires a global, multidisciplinary approach that integrates technical measures with organizational, human, legal and ethical dimensions. No single solution can provide complete protection - effective security emerges from the thoughtful integration of people, processes, and technology across all dimensions of the organization.
